Animated Films in 2022

By Sneha Patel for OIDD-2450

Introduction

I love animation. The first movie I watched in theaters was Mulan, and to this day, my favorite film is Shrek 2. Animation is an entirely different medium for entertainment, with its own rules and possibilities that differs from live-action content, but it is also capable of communicating themes as effectively as other movies. However, Animated films are often bunched together, so while most movies are categorized by content, animated films are isolated due to their style.

Films with animation are associated with being “childish,” and overall the production style is not recognized as competition to live-action productions. While remakes of animated films are common, the original films are viewed strictly under the “Animation” Genre. Only three animated films have been nominated for Best Picture. Even Shrek, the 2nd highest-grossing film in 2001, was not nominated, instead winning the newly created Best Animated Feature category.

So why do I care? This lack of appreciation for animation affects the industry. Across the board, animations are being cut more and more, often because the budget for animation is higher, making it more likely to cut financially. With distributors less likely to green light animations, it affects the content the public receives, and will lead to a smaller pool of animated entertainment.

Project Purpose & Data Acquisition

This data project aims to demonstrate the reception of animated media with respect to the entertainment industry. This project will focus on American feature films released in 2022. IMDb was scraped for movie ratings and genres as well as for domestic gross box office values, and The Numbers was scraped for revenue to date (2/27/2023). Once obtained, the gross box office data and the revenue data were both separately merged with the IMDb ratings, which were then used for analysis. For the purposes of this project, an animated film is a film that IMDb recognized under the “Animation” category.

Distributions between Animated and Non-animated

Beginning the data exploration, there are two distributions that should be analyzed for both animated films and not-animated films. For each distribution, there is a box plot overlaying the distribution for summarization.

The first distribution (above) depicts IMDb Ratings from films that were rated (i.e. a Rating above 0.00), and the bottom one depicting the Domestic Gross Box Office amounts. When it comes to the IMDb ratings, the range is significantly larger for non-animated films, most likely due to the quantity of movies. One detail to note is that when medians are measured for both subsets, the median rating for animated films is .55 higher than for non-animated films. On average, animated films were rated higher than non-animated films.

Furthermore, when examining a similar visualization for the distribution of the Gross Box Office values (above), it is important to note that the non-animated films’ distribution is right skewed. This suggests that while animated films are not always outperforming in the box office, there is a significant portion of non-animated films that “flop.” Similarly to ratings, the median gross for animated films is roughly $55.7M versus $10.2M for non-animated films.

These distributions demonstrate that on average, animated films are rated higher and gross more than non-animated films. With non-animated films being the default for cinematography, it’s worth keeping this animation advantage in mind. Animation requires a higher budget, but when it’s done, it is done well.

IMDb Rating's Effect on Revenue

Another relation to consider is if ratings affect a film’s financial success. Since the rating would impact the movie’s reception over time, financial success will be measured on revenue to date, as this is more likely to include consumers who were influenced by the movie’s reviews.

Above is a scatter plot between the revenue to date and the film’s IMDb rating. For animated and non-animated films, there is a trend line for each. In both trends, we have a positive slope, confirming that on average, as a movie’s rating increases, the revenue increases as well. When it comes to animated films, however, the slope is steeper, suggesting that for animated films, as the rating of the movie increases, the financial success for animated films is higher than non-animated films. Hence, the financial success of animated films is more positively impacted by ratings than non-animated films.

Final Thoughts

From the provided visuals and analysis, I believe that in 2022, animated movies performed better than non-animated films, particularly in critical reception and financially. While a large number of high-grossing films are not animated, these films are outliers in their category.

One limitation to acknowledge for this project is that animation makes up a small percentage of films created. As seen above, a little over 3% of films created (124 films) in 2022 are considered animation by IMDb. When these films are then merged for financial data and ratings, this value shrinks dramatically. While this makes the comparative analysis weaker between animated and non-animated works, it also emphasizes the fact that there is an underrepresentation of animation in media.

Now What?

From the above data analysis, if you are interested in exploring animated films, I have two recommendations depending on what factors are more important to you. If you are more interested in what grasped the general public’s attention, I recommend Minions: The Rise of Gru. It was the highest revenue animated film, with a revenue of over $950M, and an above average IMDb rating of 6.60. Alternatively, if you are interested in a film that is well rated, then look no further than Puss in Boots: The Last Wish. This film received a 7.90 on IMDb, and is nominated for Best Animated Feature. When I watched this movie, I laughed, I cried, and it exceeded all of my expectations.

I hope you enjoyed this data project!
